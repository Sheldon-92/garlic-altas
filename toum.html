The Multispecies Dance

The Non-Human Protagonist: Alliinase Enzyme & Garlic Proteins

Toum is a structural marvel where garlic acts as the architect. Unlike mayonnaise which uses egg yolk, Toum relies entirely on the garlic itself to hold the emulsion.

The Chemical Process: When you crush the garlic with salt, you rupture cell walls, releasing proteins and polysaccharides. These act as natural surfactants, coating millions of microscopic oil droplets to prevent them from merging. The result is a sauce that is mostly oil, yet stands up like a cloud.

Human-Tool Collaboration: The human must provide the rhythm. Adding oil too fast overwhelms the garlic proteins, breaking the emulsion. It is a negotiation between the speed of the human hand (or blender) and the unfolding capacity of the garlic molecules.

Cultural Context

In Lebanon, the stability of the Toum is a metric of the cook's skill. A broken sauce is a failure of patience. It is the inseparable partner to Shish Taouk (grilled chicken) and a staple of the Mezze table, representing the bold, unapologetic flavors of the Levant.
